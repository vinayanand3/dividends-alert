What Are Dividends? Buy the Best Dividend Stocks

Written by MarketBeat Staff
June 1, 2023

Key Points

  • Dividend stocks offer a combination of regular income and long-term growth potential.
  • Dividends can be reinvested to benefit from the compounding effect and maximize returns.
  • Diversification across sectors and industries mitigates risk when investing in dividend stocks.
  • 5 stocks we like better than Johnson & Johnson

What are dividends? Let's delve into the fascinating world of dividends, exploring what they are, how they work and why investing in the best dividend stocks can be a rewarding long-term strategy.

What is a Dividend?

What’s the dividend definition? A dividend is a sum of money paid regularly by a company to its shareholders. Beyond their financial allure, dividends provide a tangible connection between investors and the companies they choose to invest in. By investing in dividend stocks, you can grow your wealth through capital appreciation and receive a portion of the company's profits as cash payments.

Overview of Dividend Stocks

Now that you know what a dividend is, you might ask, "What are dividends in stocks"? It is essential to gain a comprehensive understanding of what stock dividends are and what their unique characteristics include. In addition to understanding the characteristics of dividend stocks, it's crucial to grasp the timeline and key dates associated with dividend payments. By familiarizing yourself with these dates, you can effectively plan and optimize your investment strategies to maximize dividend income.

Regarding dividend stocks, one category that stands out prominently is blue-chip stocks. These are shares of well-established, financially stable companies with a consistent performance history and a strong market presence. Some key characteristics of blue-chip stocks include:

  • Reliability: Blue-chip stocks are known for their reliability in generating stable earnings and cash flows. These companies often have a proven track record of weathering economic downturns and demonstrating resilience in various market conditions.
  • Dividend history: Blue-chip stocks typically have a long history of paying dividends. These companies have established a tradition of sharing some of their profits with shareholders, often through regular dividend payments.
  • Size and market dominance: Blue-chip stocks are generally large-cap companies that dominate their respective industries. Their size, market share and brand recognition often provide a competitive advantage, contributing to their stability and longevity.
  • Lower volatility: While no investment is entirely risk-free, blue-chip stocks exhibit lower volatility than smaller or riskier stocks. Their stability can make them an attractive choice for conservative investors seeking a steady income stream.

Being familiar with the key dividend dates is also crucial to invest in dividend stocks effectively. These dates help investors understand when to receive dividends and actions to take. Here are the vital dividend dates to know:

  1. Announcement date: This is the date when a company declares its intention to pay dividends. An announcement of the dividend amount typically accompanies it, as well as the record date and the payment date. The announcement date gives investors an initial heads-up and allows them to plan accordingly.
  2. Ex-dividend date: The ex-dividend date is the cutoff point set by the stock exchange to determine who is eligible to receive the upcoming dividend payment. If you own shares of stock before the ex-dividend date, you are entitled to the dividend. You will not receive the upcoming dividend if you purchase shares on or after this date.
  3. Record date: The record date, also known as the ownership date, is when the company reviews its records to determine which shareholders are eligible to receive the dividend. To be eligible, investors must own shares of the stock before the ex-dividend date.
  4. Payment date: The payment date is when the dividend is actually paid to the eligible shareholders. This is when you can expect to receive cash or additional shares if you participate in a dividend reinvestment program (DRIP).

A thorough comprehension of dividend dates empowers investors to strategically plan their investments strategically, ensuring they fulfill the necessary conditions for receiving dividend payments. Recognizing the significance of the date when dividends are paid is essential for crafting a well-rounded strategy. Consequently, conducting diligent research on dividend payment dates and comprehending their implications is paramount.

What Are Qualified Dividends?

Qualified dividends are a specific type of dividend that receive preferential tax treatment in the United States. To understand what qualified dividends are, it's essential first to grasp the concept of ordinary dividends.

Ordinary or non-qualified dividends are a corporation's regular cash or stock payments to its shareholders out of its earnings and profits. These dividends are typically taxable at the individual's ordinary income tax rates.

On the other hand, qualified dividends meet specific criteria outlined by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS), making them eligible for a lower tax rate. The primary advantage of qualified dividends is that they are subject to long-term capital gains tax rates, generally lower than ordinary income tax rates.

To be considered qualified, dividends must satisfy the following requirements:

  • Holding period: The dividend recipient must have held the underlying stock for a specific duration. For most U.S. stocks, this holding period requirement is met if the recipient holds the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period that begins 60 days before the ex-dividend date.
  • Qualified status: The dividend must be paid by a U.S. corporation or a qualified foreign corporation. To be classified as a qualified foreign corporation, the company must be incorporated in the U.S., eligible for benefits under a U.S. tax treaty, or have its stock traded on a U.S. national securities exchange.

It's important to note that not all dividends automatically qualify for the preferential tax treatment. Dividends from sources such as real estate investment trusts (REITs), mutual funds, certain exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and dividends on employee stock options are generally not considered qualified dividends.

Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRIPs)

Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRIPs) are investment programs offered by companies that allow shareholders to automatically reinvest their cash dividends to purchase additional shares or fractional shares of the company's stock. DRIPs provide a convenient and automated method for investors to compound their returns over time by reinvesting dividends rather than receiving them in cash.

When investors enroll in a DRIP, they permit the company to use their dividend payments to buy more shares on their behalf. Instead of receiving the cash dividends directly, the dividends are reinvested in the company's stock at the prevailing market price or a discounted price provided by the company.

DRIPs offer several benefits to investors. Firstly, they enable compounding returns. By reinvesting dividends, shareholders acquire more shares, and as those additional shares generate dividends, those dividends are also reinvested. This compounding effect increases share ownership and potential future dividend income.

DRIPs also facilitate dollar-cost averaging. Through regular automatic dividend payments, investors acquire shares at different price points. This strategy helps to mitigate the impact of short-term market volatility.

Another advantage of DRIPs is cost efficiency. Many DRIPs allow investors to acquire additional shares without incurring brokerage fees or commissions, resulting in cost savings, especially for those who make frequent smaller purchases.

Enrolling in a DRIP can be done directly through the company or a brokerage. Some companies offer direct stock purchase plans (DSPPs) that allow investors to buy shares directly from the company and automatically reinvest dividends. Alternatively, many brokerages provide DRIP services, allowing investors to reinvest dividends from multiple companies in a single account.

When considering DRIPs, it's essential to consider the tax implications. Although investors do not receive cash dividends through DRIPs, they are still subject to income tax on the value of the reinvested dividends. Additionally, the reinvested dividends increase the investor's cost basis, potentially reducing future capital gains tax liability.

Furthermore, investors should evaluate the flexibility of DRIPs. Some plans allow additional cash purchases to supplement dividend reinvestment, while others only reinvest dividends without additional investments. It's also essential to review any fees associated with DRIP participation, such as enrollment fees or account maintenance fees. Additionally, some DRIPs offer discounts on shares purchased.

Overview of Dividend-Paying Companies 

In the dynamic investing world, dividend-paying companies hold a special place, offering investors a unique combination of regular income and potential long-term growth strategies. These companies have a proven track record of distributing a portion of their earnings to shareholders as dividends, making them an attractive option for those seeking a reliable income stream from their investments. 

Dividend-paying companies exhibit distinct qualities that make them appealing to income-oriented investors. Their stable cash flows, established track records, financial strength and presence in mature industries contribute to their ability to distribute dividends to shareholders consistently. These companies prioritize returning value to their investors and often have a history of maintaining or increasing dividend payments over time.

Reasons to Invest in Dividend Stocks

Investing in dividend stocks has long been favored by investors seeking a balanced approach that combines potential capital appreciation with regular income. Dividend stocks offer unique advantages that make them attractive investment options for many individuals, from conservative investors looking for stable returns to income-focused investors aiming to generate a consistent cash flow. Let's explore the compelling reasons to invest in dividend stocks and provide a comprehensive understanding of their benefits.

Reason 1: Income Generation

One of the primary reasons investors choose dividend stocks is the ability to generate a steady income stream. Dividend-paying companies distribute a portion of their earnings directly to shareholders, providing a reliable income source that can supplement other sources of income, such as salaries or retirement funds. Some strategies include dividend harvesting and dividend capture to maximize your returns without holding the stock for long periods. 

Reason 2: Potential for Dividend Growth

Investing in dividend stocks allows investors to participate in a company's growth story. Established companies with a history of increasing their dividend payments over time can allow investors to benefit from growing dividend income as the company's earnings and profitability improve.

Reason 3: Stability and Defensive Characteristics

Dividend stocks, particularly those from mature and stable industries, often exhibit defensive characteristics. These companies are less volatile than growth stocks, making them attractive to risk-averse investors seeking stability and downside protection during market downturns.

Reason 4: Compounding Returns

Reinvesting dividends from dividend stocks can lead to compounding returns over time. By utilizing dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs) or manually reinvesting dividends, investors can purchase additional shares, which, in turn, generate more dividends. This compounding effect can significantly enhance long-term wealth accumulation.

Reason 5: Potential for Capital Appreciation

While dividend stocks are known for their income-generating capabilities, they can also offer the potential for capital appreciation. Companies that consistently pay dividends are often well-established and financially strong, which can contribute to their stock prices appreciating over time, leading to capital gains for investors.

Reason 6: Favorable Tax Treatment

Dividends may be subject to favorable tax treatment compared to other forms of investment income, such as interest or short-term capital gains. For example, qualified dividends in the United States are subject to lower tax rates than ordinary income, making dividend stocks an attractive option for tax-conscious investors.

Reason 7: Portfolio Diversification

Dividend stocks can provide diversification benefits to an investment portfolio. By adding dividend-paying stocks from various sectors and industries, investors can reduce overall portfolio risk and potentially enhance returns through a balanced mix of income-focused investments and growth-oriented assets.

Reason 8: Historical Outperformance

Historically, dividend-paying stocks have delivered competitive returns compared to non-dividend-paying stocks. Dividend-focused strategies have often outperformed the broader market, providing investors with the potential for attractive total returns over the long term.

Reason 9: Alignment with Value Investing Principles

Dividend stocks often align with the principles of value investing, which focus on identifying undervalued stocks and investing in companies with strong fundamentals. Dividend payments can indicate a company's financial health and management's confidence in its future prospects.

Reason 10: Mitigating Inflation Risk

Dividend stocks have the potential to act as a hedge against inflation. As companies increase their dividend payments over time, investors may benefit from a rising income stream that can help maintain purchasing power in the face of inflationary pressures.

Reason 11: Potential for Passive Income

Dividend stocks can serve as a source of passive income, allowing investors to generate consistent cash flow without actively selling assets. This passive income can be particularly beneficial for individuals who rely on investment returns to cover living expenses or achieve financial goals.

Reason 12: Dividend Stability

Dividend-paying companies often prioritize maintaining their dividend payments, even during economic downturns. Their commitment to regular distributions reflects a level of financial stability and management confidence in the company's ability to generate sustainable profits over time.

Reason 13: Favorable for Retirement Planning

Retirees and those planning for retirement favor dividend stocks due to their income-generating nature. Consistent dividend payments can provide a reliable income stream to support retirement expenses and potentially reduce reliance on other income sources.

Reason 14: Rebalancing Opportunities

Dividend payments can create opportunities for portfolio rebalancing. As dividend stocks generate income, investors can reinvest those funds in other assets to maintain an optimal asset allocation and diversification strategy.

Reason 15: Transparent and Tangible Returns

Dividends are tangible returns that investors can observe and measure directly. Unlike the potential gains from capital appreciation, which are realized only upon selling shares, dividends provide investors with a clear indication of the income they receive from their investments.

Investing in dividend stocks offers a multitude of compelling reasons to investors. From income generation and potential dividend growth to stability, compounding returns and favorable tax treatment, dividend stocks have proven to be an attractive choice for various investment strategies. The combination of regular income, the potential for capital appreciation and the psychological and tangible benefits they provide make dividend stocks a valuable addition to your investment portfolios.

Best Dividend Stocks

When it comes to dividend stocks, not all options are equal. Price, dividend frequency and payout size vary across different stocks. Rather than seeking a universally "right" or "wrong" dividend stock, the key is to find the ones that align best with your investment goals. 

Whether you aim to generate regular cash flow or diversify your portfolio with affordable dividend stocks, the choice will depend on your specific needs and preferences.

While many companies are known for their consistent dividend payments, the notion of the "best" dividend stocks is not static. Like any investment, timing plays a crucial role. The stocks considered the best dividend performers in the past may not hold the same status in the future. Therefore, analyzing the most up-to-date data available for those seeking the best cheap dividend stocks is essential.

Moreover, the timing of purchasing dividend stocks is critical. It may be tempting to buy a stock immediately after a dividend announcement. However, if you purchase the ex-dividend date, you won't receive the dividend. Instead, the previous owner of the stock will be entitled to the dividend payout.

Standard criteria are often used to assist investors in evaluating dividend stocks. This list includes companies that meet specific parameters frequently employed by investors for assessing dividend stocks. The requirements include dividend yields greater than 3%, payout ratios of less than 75% (or less than 100% for REITs), a three-year average dividend growth rate of at least 5% and a minimum market capitalization of $1 billion.

By considering these factors and conducting thorough research, investors can identify dividend stocks that align with their investment objectives, helping them navigate the dynamic landscape of dividend investing with confidence and a data-driven approach. Dividend Tax Rate 

The dividend tax rate refers to the percentage of tax applied to the income received from dividends. It is an important consideration for investors as it affects their overall tax liabilities. Dividend tax rates can vary based on the tax jurisdiction and the individual's tax bracket. Dividends are typically categorized as qualified or ordinary, with qualified dividends often subject to lower tax rates than ordinary ones. 

When considering investment options, blue-chip funds can be an attractive route. Blue-chip funds invest in well-established companies with stable earnings and reliable dividend payments. These funds offer several benefits, including stability, reliable dividend income, diversification, potential for capital appreciation and professional management. By investing in blue-chip funds, investors can gain exposure to financially sound and established companies, providing stability and potential long-term growth. 

Furthermore, blue-chip stocks often pay consistent dividends, making them appealing to income-focused investors. Blue-chip funds offer diversification benefits by investing in stocks from various sectors, reducing the risk associated with individual companies or sectors. The expertise of professional fund managers in managing blue-chip funds can provide added confidence and guidance for investors.

How Do Dividends Affect a Stock's Share Price?

The impact of dividends on a stock's share price is significant and can be understood through various factors. Dividends can influence a stock's share price due to their impact on the company's financial health and the market's perception of its value. When a company declares and pays dividends to its shareholders, it signifies that it is generating profits and has surplus cash available for distribution. 

This regular income stream from dividends attracts investors seeking income-generating investments. The demand for stocks offering dividends can increase, resulting in a higher demand for the company's shares and potentially driving up the stock's share price. Conversely, suppose a company reduces or eliminates its dividend payments. In that case, it might disappoint income-focused investors and lead to a decrease in demand for the stock, potentially causing a decline in its share price. 

Moreover, the announcement and payment of dividends can instill investor confidence, reflecting positively on the company's financial stability and prospects. This positive sentiment can drive up demand for the stock, leading to an increase in its share price. However, it's important to note that dividends alone do not determine a stock's share price. Other factors, such as overall market conditions, company performance, industry trends and investor sentiment, also play crucial roles in influencing stock prices.

How to Buy Dividend Stocks

Buying dividend stocks is a strategic investment approach that can provide investors with regular income and potential long-term growth. Understanding the process of purchasing dividend stocks is essential for investors looking to incorporate them into their investment portfolios. Let's review the steps to buying a dividend stock. 

Step 1: Determine your investment goals and risk tolerance.

Before buying any dividend stock, you want to ask yourself, "What are dividend stocks that align with my investment goals"? Clarifying investment goals and assessing risk tolerance is crucial to any investment strategy. Consider whether your specific dividend investment strategy requires regular income from your dividend stocks monthly, quarterly, or annually. Also, consider capital appreciation or a combination of capital appreciation and regular income payments. Evaluate your tolerance for market volatility and determine the level of risk you are comfortable with. These factors will influence your investment strategy and the types of dividend stocks you choose.

Step 2: Research dividend-paying companies.

Conduct thorough research on dividend-paying companies to identify potential investment opportunities. Look for companies with consistent dividend payments, strong financial performance and a sustainable dividend policy. Consider factors such as the company's earnings growth, payout ratio, dividend yield and stability of dividend payments over time. Also, consider which sectors fit into your risk assessment. For instance, if you want your portfolio to be more defensive, consider including some of the top consumer staples stocks to increase your defensive positions. This research will help you build a list of dividend stocks that align with your investment goals.

Step 3: Evaluate dividend metrics.

Evaluate key dividend metrics to assess the attractiveness of potential dividend stocks. Pay attention to metrics such as dividend yield, which indicates the annual dividend payment as a percentage of the stock price. Consider researching how to find a good dividend yield. A higher yield may indicate a higher income potential, but it is essential to consider the company's financial health and sustainability of dividend payments. Additionally, analyze the company's dividend growth rate and payout ratio, which provide insights into the company's ability to increase dividends over time and the proportion of earnings distributed as dividends.

Step 4: Consider sector and industry factors.

Evaluate stock sectors and industry factors when selecting dividend stocks. Different sectors and industries have varying levels of stability, growth potential and sensitivity to economic conditions. Assess the current economic environment and consider sectors likely to perform well in that context. Diversifying across sectors can help mitigate risk and enhance the stability of your dividend stock portfolio.

Step 5: Open an investment account.

To buy dividend stocks, you must open an investment account with a brokerage firm. Research different brokerage options and select one that offers competitive fees, a user-friendly platform and access to a wide range of dividend-paying stocks. Follow the account opening process, which typically involves providing personal information, completing necessary forms and funding your account.

Step 6: Place buy orders.

Once your investment account is set up and funded, you can place buy orders for the selected dividend stocks. Specify the number of shares you wish to purchase and your purchase price. You can choose between market orders (executed at the prevailing market price) or limit orders (set a specific price you are willing to buy). Monitor the market closely to ensure your orders are executed at favorable prices.

Step 7: Monitor and manage your dividend stock portfolio.

After buying dividend stocks, monitoring and managing your portfolio is crucial. Stay updated on the performance and news related to the companies in your portfolio. Review financial reports, dividend announcements and any significant developments that may impact the companies or the broader market. Regularly assess your portfolio's performance, make necessary adjustments and consider reinvesting dividends or rebalancing your holdings to align with your investment goals.

By following these steps and conducting thorough research, investors can successfully navigate the process of buying dividend stocks. Building a diversified portfolio of high-quality dividend-paying companies can provide a consistent income stream and potential long-term growth, supporting your financial goals and aspirations.

FAQs

We understand that investors may still have questions regarding this investment approach. Let's address some common queries and provide further clarity on dividend investing. By addressing these frequently asked questions, we hope to equip you with the knowledge and confidence to make informed decisions regarding incorporating dividend stocks into your investment strategy. 

How does a dividend work?

First, it is essential to understand "What are stock dividends"? Dividends are a distribution of a company's earnings to its shareholders utilizing the dividend formula, typically in the form of cash or additional shares of stock. When a company declares a dividend, it sets a specific amount to be paid per share or as a percentage of the stock's price. The dividend is then paid out to shareholders on a predetermined date. The amount each shareholder receives is proportional to their ownership stake in the company. Dividends can provide investors with a regular income stream and potentially enhance long-term returns.

What is an example of a dividend?

An example of a dividend can be seen in Johnson & Johnson (NYSE: JNJ), a well-known multinational healthcare corporation. J&J regularly pays dividends to its shareholders as a way to distribute a portion of its profits. For instance, if an investor owns 100 shares of J&J and the company declares a dividend of $1 per share, the investor would receive a dividend payment of $1,000. Dividends are typically paid per share, and the amount can vary depending on the company's profitability and dividend policy. 

How are dividends paid on shares?

Dividends on shares are paid out to shareholders differently, depending on the company's preference and the investor's chosen method. The most common forms of dividend payment are cash dividends and stock dividends. Cash dividends are paid directly to shareholders' brokerage accounts or sent via check. On the other hand, stock dividends involve distributing additional shares of the company's stock to shareholders. The company's dividend policy determines the exact payment method and timing of dividends and is typically communicated to shareholders through dividend announcements and official documentation.
